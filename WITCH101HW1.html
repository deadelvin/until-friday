How the witch behaveth

Friends, there is a difference between a witch and a poisoner, or venificae, which in Hebrew is Chasaph; the latter is what Exodus 22 is actually referring to in the translation ‚Äúthou shalt not suffer a witch [poisoner/venificae/chasaph] to live‚Äù (90). Fellow countrymen I warn thee: the witch not only flies through the air, but, through her bargains with the devil, can transfer corn or grass from one field to an-other; she harms the children and cattle with words and rhyming charms; and bewitches butter, cheese, and ale such as that it will not come ‚Äúno matter how long you churn‚Äù (xxii, 8). The punishment for such is death; though on first offense, perhaps imprisonment or forfeit of goods or cattle (xxxii); perhaps hanging, exorcism, or burning (xxxiv); perhaps forcible confession (xxxix); and she shall be bound and tortured until such confession occurs (17/18).

Whether ye believe the witch hath power of her own, or whether ye believe the devil works his charms through her (ix), ‚Äòtis all the same.

Shapeshifting

The witch is a shapeshifter, and chiefly a Lycanthrope. Scot notes that Bodin, whose works are central to these witch hunts, says that it is natural for a girl to become a boy, any female may be turned into a male 4, but it is unnatural for humans to become wolves, though it is a real phenomena, attained by means of ointment (72). At the three year mark he was with the witch out in town, and stayed behind her a bit, finding himself near a churchyard, he knelt in prayer, and the witch came back to find him and beat him to follow her again. Merchants saw this, and accused her of witchcraft, and so she was brought forth before a judge, who examined her and made her confess, and she promised to ‚Äúrestore him to his old/ shape‚Äù, which she did (76).
